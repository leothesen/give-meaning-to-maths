neurone disease -- correctly termed Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis - but
this did not stop him investigating, and from succeeding in his studies

time moving backwards, and of 11 dimensions. The key concept was Gravity
and its role in studying cosmology.

He studied his Cosmology -- explanation of the Universe - at Oxford
University, then to Cambridge, where he held Newton's chair as Lucasion
Professor of Mathematics.

Obviously his ideas will be questioned, but they have been justified by
rigorous Mathematics. No Scientist can claim to be absolutely correct.
Well, he should not, as he deals with Hypotheses -- that is 'Proposals'
or tenets of what is the least criticisible opinion at that moment in
time. Many have taken his hypotheses as facts.

The scientist knows that someone will question his hypothesis and then
proposes an alternate less criticible postulation. And thus 'knowledge'
expands.

Sir Isaac Newton (1642 to 1726) created what was to become Calculus as a
way of relating the concepts of Physical Science. He saw reality as
Matter (Mass in kilograms), Distance (metre), Time (seconds) where these
concepts related.

**Changing position is distance**, or displacement if in a set
direction.

**Changing distance (displacement) is speed** -- or velocity in a set
direction.

**Changing velocity is acceleration**, one example being the force of
gravity -- hence the apocryphal story of the apple falling at a constant
rate of acceleration.

He saw Mass accelerating as Force. Force (in Newtons) = Mass times
Acceleration.

Work, or Energy is a Force moving a Distance. W = Fs (in Joule).

Power is the rate of doing Work (in Watt). Power = $\frac{Work}{Time}$
('the rate of doing work').

\* All in the physical world was seen as the relationships between Mass,
Distance, Time. These were Newton's Absolutes -- by which everything
could be explained and measured. This implied that the Universe was
Static and bound by set laws. There was a 'Certainty'. Remember this
sentence. Is there a Certainty?

\* Machines had operated on the principles of physics -- not on
electricity nor other phenomena. Einstein (1879 to 1955) determined the
ratio of Energy to Mass as the square of the velocity of light. He wrote
it as E = m$c^{2}$. It created a more dynamic world, increasingly
obsessed by Energy -- hence the Atomic bomb evolved!

\* In theorising on the Universe, Einstein made 'an error' and concluded
that the Universe is static but Russian scientist Alex Friedmann (1888
to 1925) devised formulae that showed that the universe is dynamic -
forever expanding, as La Maitre had said. Einstein saw the controlling
factors to be Relative not Absolute.

***Jacob Lund's Exposition on* Recurring Digits Problem:**

Every rational number can be represented by either a decimal expansion
that ends such as $\frac{1}{2} = 0,5$ ; $\frac{1}{4} = 0,25$ ;
$\frac{1}{5} = 0,2$

Or by one with some infinitely repeating pattern of digits, for example:

$\frac{1}{3} = 0,33333333\ldots = 0,\overline{3}$ ;
$\frac{1}{7} = 0,14285714\ldots = 0,\overline{142857}$

But how can we predict the number of digits that will be repeated for
the reciprocal of any natural number n? Furthermore, what will those
digits be?

We start off by assuming a well-known identity based in limits:

$$1 = 0,99999999\ldots = 0,\overline{9}$$

Dividing both sides by 9, we can see that:

$$\frac{1}{9} = 0,11111111\ldots = 0,\overline{1}$$

Similarly, it can be shown that:

$$\frac{1}{99} = 0,01010101\ldots = 0,\overline{01}$$

And in general:

$$\frac{1}{10^{x} - 1} = 0.\overline{\underset{x - 1\ zeros}{\overset{00000\ldots}{︸}}1}$$

that has a total of x recurring digits.

Next, if the number has prime factors of 2 or 5, we must divide them
out! A half and a fifth both have decimal expansions which don't repeat,
so factoring them out of an expression shouldn't affect the number of
recurring digits. Take the example of 1/6 which has one repeating digit:

$$\frac{1}{6} = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{3}$$

$$\therefore\frac{1}{6} = (0,5) \times (0,33333333\ldots)$$

$$\therefore\frac{1}{6} = (0,5) \times (0,3)\  + \ (0,5) \times (0,03)\  + \ (0,5) \times (0,003)\cdots$$

$$\therefore\frac{1}{6} = 0,15\  + 0,015\  + \ 0,0015\cdots$$

$$\therefore\frac{1}{6} = 0,16666666\ldots = 0,1\overline{6}$$

Since we don't care what the digits are, only how many there are we can
safely divide out 2 and 5 (or any multiples of 2 and 5) until our new
number n has neither of these as factors.

Then we assume:

$$nm = 10^{x} - 1$$

for some m and x which are both natural numbers. This is reasonable to
assume as when x becomes larger, there must at some point be a value of
x for which this is true.

Rearranging this relationship, we get:

$$\frac{1}{n} = \frac{m}{10^{x} - 1}$$

Subbing in our equation from earlier we get:

$$\frac{1}{n} = m \times (0.\overline{\underset{x - 1\ zeros}{\overset{00000\ldots}{︸}}1})$$

Therefore, we can see that $\frac{1}{\ n}$ has x recurring digits (where
x is the smallest natural number for which this relationship is true).

Additionally, we can predict what these digits are by calculating the
value of m: For example, 7x142 857 = 999 999, and therefore the
recurring digits of 7 are:

$$\frac{1}{7} = 142857 \times (0.\overline{000001})$$

$$\therefore\frac{1}{7} = \ 0,\overline{142857}$$

Similarly:
