**MEETING KARL GAUSS TOPIC 35**

![](/assets/patterns-in-numbers-image10.jpeg)

*Image 71*

**Karl Gauss** (b 1777- d 1855) is regarded as the Prince of
Mathematicians. From a poor family, he developed many theorems in
Mathematics - like the frequency of Prime Numbers - and he started many
branches of Mathematics.

Of'times, he would have proven a point, but not published it. Someone
would hear of it, develop it a bit and walk into fame and recognition.
It did not bother Gauss.

One day, when aged 6, his teacher was telling the class about fairies
painting the leaves green. He interrupted to give a lecture on what we
later called 'photosynthesis'. So he was told to go to the back of the
room and add all the numbers 1 to 100. He instantly called out 5050. How
did he do that?

1 + 2 + 3 + ....... + 50 + 51 + ...... + 97 + 98 + 99 + 100

All he did was to fold the numbers over:

100 + 1 ; 99 + 2 ; 98 + 3 : 97 + 4 ; ....etc. .... to 51 + 50.

Being 50 pairs of 101, being 5050.

Fooling Around with a Gaussian Theory:

We saw earlier that the TRIANGLE Numbers are

1 ; 3 ; 6 ; 10 ; 15 ; 21 ; 28 ; 36 ; 45 ; 55 ; 66 ; 78 ; 91 ....

Whose general formula is $\frac{n(n + 1)}{2}$

***He claimed that any number could be written as the sum of 3 (at most)
triangular numbers.***

Two easy demonstrations 40 = 36 + 3 + 1 and 100 = 91 + 6 + 3.

- To check 40, I looked for the biggest Triangle number just less
  than 40. Knowing the formula was $\frac{n(n + 1)}{2}$ , I fed in 8, so
  $\frac{8\ x\ 9}{2}$ = 36. I just needed 4 more, being the 3 and the 1.

- To explore 100. I threw in 12 to find that the 12^th^ Triangle number
  was 78, thus needed 22 more, being 21 and 1. Had I fed in 13, the
  13^th^ Triangle number would have been 91, that just needed the 6 + 3
  to get to 100.

- So how would you investigate the number 2020 as the sum of 3 Triangle
  Numbers?

Sure Gauss had a great complicated way of proving this with ALL numbers
-- easy to him though. His genius was to propose the question not just
to get the answer.
