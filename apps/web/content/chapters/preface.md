A short note on why this book exists, and what I hope it does.

For thirty-four years I taught mathematics in one school or another, and for
thirty-four years I had roughly the same conversation, every September, with a
fresh batch of fourteen-year-olds. *Sir, when am I ever going to use this?*
Sometimes the question was hostile and sometimes it was sad and once or twice
it was earnest, but it was always there.

This book is the long answer I never had time to give in a forty-minute
lesson. It is not a textbook. It is not a memoir. It is something in between —
the arguments I would make if I had the room to make them properly, and a
handful of problems I have come back to, over the years, because they keep
teaching me something new.

I have written it for three sorts of reader. First, the pupil who is
unconvinced. Second, the adult who decided long ago that they were "not a
maths person" and would now, gently, like to reconsider. Third, the teacher —
because the teaching of mathematics is a craft, and crafts are passed on by
talking shop.

If you take only one thing from these pages, let it be this: mathematics is
not a list of tricks to memorise. It is a way of paying attention. The rest of
the book is a long argument for that single sentence.

— P. B., Cape Town, 2025
