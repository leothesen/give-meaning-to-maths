IN PERSPECTIVE

This compilation, suitable for any High School pupil or Tutor or Student Teacher \- or for those who would want to look back, or who would like to expose a class of students to ‘something different’, ‘something more meaningful’, ‘something richer and deeper’, is very deliberately not comprehensive nor written to suit any specific agenda. It is NOT a textbook – on the contrary. It is not a text for Competitions. It is a beginning of the Search by YOU for the Meaning behind Mathematics that will benefit YOU – the thinker.

It is for you or those who depend on you, to be enriched, to be exposed to and to be informed \- to get excited. It is to whet your appetite. To encourage you to investigate and to think wider. To give you background, and to keep you asking “Why?“  “Why?”  “Why?” It is for YOU to find that one Pebble that is ‘The Pearl of Great Price’ – that you can develop and thus be motivated as a thinker giving ***“Meaning to Maths”.** It is possible to “Like Maths\!”*

*You will be exposed to Pebbles that turn out to be Pearls that will give Meaning to Mathematics, and in that, YOU will be enriched. Mathematics will contribute to your development – if you go for it.*  
***“A Pebble In the Streamlet Scant***  
***Has Turned the Course of Many a River.***  
***The Dewdrop on the Baby Plant,***  
***Might Warp the Giant Oak Forever.”***

*To ask ‘Why?’ ‘Why?’ ‘Why?’ To entice and to provoke you to look for more on the Internet, Google, Wikipedia, Numberphile, PInterest, the superb Veritasium, Brilliant, wherever – there is so much out there that was not available to your parents. Go for it.* 

*Add a Sense of Adventure. It will not be difficult to Venture to things New to you nor Different that will Entrench, Enrich and Stabilise the Basics.* 

*Nothing beats excellent preparation by a tutor, especially in the current era, where one can consult so much on the Internet, et al.*  
*You will even find that Mathematics is ‘Incomplete’, and you will wonder why? Is it written in the skies waiting to be found?  Is it discovered or does it evolve?  How does it advance?*   
*You will find discourses in this book – to show you that you can direct the critical thinking developed in order to opine on world events.*

*Where to and why? Is it ‘Certain’? Is it ‘Uncertain’?*   
*Must every issue be Proven?  Can each Conjecture be Proven?*

*Respect the interests and questions of others – even the oddest of questions. You only need ‘a pebble in the streamlet scant …’ to change your entire thinking. Often an insignificant question initiates new directions. Life itself can find Meaning. If the child proposes, postulates, tests and argues, then you will be amazed at what emerges. It can be called ‘Mathematising’, as opposed to simply  ‘doing Maths’ that can so easily become an obsession to too many.*

*Special thanks to J.P. Le Roux previously at Cedar College, Kenilworth and now with Curro, for the impetus and the Foreword. I first met J.P. twenty years ago, when his beard was fresh and he was teaching at a private school in the Karoo town of Oudtshoorn. He just lapped up thinking differently. His subsequent Doctorate is in Mathematics Education. He travels to international symposia. He has made his luck. He found the Pearl of Great Price.*

His star pupil, Jacob Lund, gave great input on the more advanced investigative ‘material’. An ex-pupil of mine from 1975 to 1979, now living in Brazil, Shaune Cooke threw in his support to have the book finalised. He too found that Pearl. Another past pupil, of the same vintage, Julie Stevenson (nee Reid), did the proof-reading.

***FOREWORD***  
                                       **Give Meaning to Mathematics**  
J.P. le Roux  \-  Executive Head Curro,  Brackenfell, Cape.  
*Questions have been asked over the last couple of decades about the relevance and importance of students learning Mathematics and why so much emphasis has been placed on Mathematics Education. Although Mathematics curricula over the world differ vastly in composition and depth, it is the promise of developing students with a skillset and mindset associated with rigorous, methodical or often out-of-the-box problem-solving capacity that unites educators and researchers of Mathematics. *  
*As is the case with curricula, views of the essence of Mathematics Education differ greatly in research, but there is one aspect of Mathematics Education that research agrees on: the impact that a gifted Mathematics teacher and the use of the right resources can make on learning Mathematics. Teachers who are life-long learners. Teachers who are committed to learning how to learn, who can reflect and engage with metacognition, and who know how to unlearn and relearn. Teachers who learn with their students. Teachers who have growth mindsets and who are committed to the growth of every student. Teachers who are not easily satisfied with superficial explanations and who have the highest expectations of every one of their students. Teachers who work tirelessly to understand what is at the core when students battle with certain topics, and who are determined to find a breakthrough so that students can get that ‘Ah-hah moment’. “Wow\!”. Teachers who are driven to develop the Mathematical Proficiency of their students, and understand that it is the combination of conceptual understanding, procedural fluency, adaptive reasoning, strategic competence and productive disposition that needs to be enhanced. Teachers who exhibit a contagious academic curiosity when attempting an unseen problem. Peter Bishop has been such an Educator of Mathematics.*  
*It was Peter Bishop’s approach to teaching Mathematics and his beliefs and perceptions of the holistic learning of Mathematics that inspired me. It was how he challenged his thinking, to his determination to always find more creative ways of solving his questions and curiosities that challenged my worldview of teaching and learning. Always interrogating his conceptual understanding of the inter-related connections of concepts, and being unsatisfied with lazy thinking and approaches, even when the solutions to problems were known, makes him a master educator. I remember a moment so well moderating mathematics school-based portfolios in March 2008 at his house in George, that made an everlasting impact on me. Peter discussed one of his projects on the complex applications of the Golden Ratio (relative to The Stock Exchange) that he did with his students. When I asked him why he chose to focus on a topic outside the scope of the curriculum, he responded in his passionate voice that for him it is was not a question of the type of Mathematical content to focus on, but what the skills the students can learn to apply the concepts of those topics that interested them. He wanted to focus on an area that every student can relate to and appreciate, and that will develop lateral thinking, grit and methodical problem-solving skills when they engage with the content. He talked about how some of his students were more interested in fashion design, art, dance, gymnastics, drumming and surfing than Mathematics. So look for the Mathematics in that interest\!*   
*How these students only realized afterwards the higher-order mathematical problems that they were able to solve, all because he made the mathematical problem relevant to them. He challenged me on the dangers of textbook teaching methods and treating Mathematics as an isolated subject matter. He inspired me to commit fully to develop the thinking and confidence of each of my students, to own the learning that takes place in every lesson and to have high expectations of every student. He changed my worldview of teaching and learning.*   
*Peter’s quest to develop young minds and old souls to think laterally has always resulted in making Mathematics fun and meaningful.*   
*To purposely expose his learners to grapple with higher-order topical mathematical content, and then to Give Deep Meaning to the Mathematics, is such a gift of his. Most Mathematics teachers will agree that this is a necessity, but very few highly experienced teachers I know can manage to create content that will engage learners. We know there is nothing more dangerous than a disengaged learner. This book will engage teachers and learners to dig deeper, enhance their proficiency and strengthen their relationship with the learning process, while at the same time igniting their creativity and curiosity. What a Blessing PeeBee and this book are to strengthen the wider Mathematics community\!*
