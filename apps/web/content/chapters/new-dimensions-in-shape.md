**GEOMETRY QUESTIONS FROM WAY BACK TOPIC 147**

Euclidean Geometry was synthesised by the Alexandrian Greek Euclid
(circa 300 years before Christ). He did not propose a model of the
universe. This was done by the Indians and Arabian world who developed
Trigonometry to understand the 3-dimensional world they encountered in
the sky. Euclid developed rather a link between what can appear on a
plane (a flat surface) and with solids. It is actually a system of logic
that enables us to deduce logical conclusions to stated premises. The
skill of " If .... Then .... ". He needed a starting point -- and the
tools of logic.

He proposed 5 axioms (self-evident facts) from which he synthesized what
he we know as Euclidean Geometry. With Einstein's different vision,
other Geometries have been postulated. We will look at these.

These 5 axioms embraced that from a POINT, a line can be drawn, and if
that line moved in the same direction, it would be straight. If one
point of a line rotated around the other that was fixed, a circle would
be drawn. Another axiom was that all right angles were equal. Of course,
he worked in the 2-Dimensional world.

But the famous, or infamous FIFTH POSTULATE was to prove a weakness. He
stated that if 2 lines were cut by a third, then if the 2 inner angles
added to less than 180°, those 2 lines would meet. Others were
to re-word this postulate as: "If a line passed through a point not on a
line, so that it any point on that path is equidistant from the line,
then the 2 lines resulting are parallel".

This was to prove to be the Achilles heel. Thereafter Euclid went into
logically provable tenets about triangles, quadrilaterals, etc.

**THE WEAK SPOT IN EUCLIDEAN GEOMETRY TOPIC 148**

Euclid attempted to create a completely logical system that 'reflected
the physical world'- the flat earth, the work on paper -- in 2
Dimensions, rather than the astronomical world as favoured in India and
the Middle East. To his credit, he did not purport to be a cosmologist,
but rather to be a Logician.

You have read about The 5th Postulate as the weakness in Euclid. Going
on to parallel lines, he took another jump -- called a logical leap.

We hold that if any 2 parallel lines are cut by a transversal

\(i\) the corresponding angles are equal (ii) the alternate angles are
equal and

\(iii\) the co-interior angles are supplementary. He did not PROVE these
from any earlier premise -- but rather as a closed system. This had
implications, and eventually he was to be questioned. Never was it
proven that the 'corresponding angles are equal'. It has been stated,
and used to proceed to other 2 statements.

All Mathematics teachers survive the questioning child by making the
pupils learn the Theorem off by heart. So the next generation misses out
questioning the tenets proposed. His only way of silencing the critic is
to claim that this is a closed system.

> If the corresponding angles are equal, then the other 2 facts are
> provable.
>
> If the alternate angles are equal, then the other 2 facts are
> provable.
>
> If the co-interior angles are supplementary, then the other 2 facts
> are equal.

But Euclid is forever criticised by that FIFTH POSTULATE: If one had a
straight line, and a point off that line, then the line drawn through
the point, equidistant from the given line, would show that the 2 lines
are parallel on to infinity. Was he correct? Think? It was to be
regarded as a weak spot in his arguments. Why? Why? Why?

> **NON-EUCLIDEAN GEOMETRY**

So it was not sacred that 2 lines equidistant from each other are
parallel. It was to be found that the angles of a triangle necessarily
add to 180$°.$ Nor that the circumference of a circle was
2$\pi$r. Apparently. Euclidean lines had no curvature. It was drawn on
flat paper. The frame of reference was flat -- a piece of 2-dimensional
paper (Image 343). All that is true if the surface was 2-Dimensional --
on flat paper.

But are surfaces only 'FLAT"? Think of a sphere. Think of a horse
saddle. Can we make those statements on parallel lines, and on
triangles?

On a **spherical** shape, like the 'global' earth (Image 344) that was
being increasingly mapped, and where air flights were planned, that path
could look 'straight' if you look from above, but if you viewed from the
side, it was curved.

To go from one airport to another, the shortest distance was not from
mapping, but by tracking 'great circles'. Parallel lines (like the lines
of longitude) would converge at one point (North or South Pole), so
there were no parallel lines.

The angles of a triangle swelled to be more than 180°. The
circumference of a circle would be less than 2$\pi$r. The
'perpendicular' distance between 2 points, was not constant, but reduced
as the lines converged to a point. Thus it was called Elliptical
(Spherical) Geometry. Lines on an Elliptical shape had a positive
curvature, converging -- as we see at the North and South Poles.

The Islamic Mathematicians tried to put Euclid on a more solid basis.
Ibn Al-Haytham (965-1039) redefined parallelism as equidistance. Then
Omar Khayyam, as Umar Ibn Ibrahim al-Khayyami (1048-1123) took a
different approach, before the ideas of Nasir al-Din al-Tusi (1201-1274)
reached England's John Wallis and the Italian Girolamo Saccheri
(1676-1733), who asked the questions -- but gave up pushing further.

There are many Geometries. There are primarily 3 that reflect the
2-Dimensional world and 8 that reflect the 3-Dimensional World. It
depends on what our model of the physical world is -- mainly
combinations of the basic three below. At school level, we sweat on the
Euclidean Geometry. Why?

Taking that third type of surface -- the horse saddle -- '**the
hyperbola'** - if one tried to draw parallel lines (equally distant from
point to the line) the lines would not stay equally far apart (as
expected in Euclidean Geometry), nor would they converge ( as in
**Elliptical** Geometry), but they would diverge.

On these **3 surfaces**, respectively, the sum of the
angles would equal$\ 180°,$exceed 180°, or be less than
180° - as seen in these sketches!

**Re: Parallel lines:** Given a parallel line, and a point
P away from that line AB, Euclid had it that there was 1 and only one
line that could be drawn through P parallel to AB, and the distance
between the 2 lines was constant infinitely. Try to draw 'parallel'
lines on each of these 3 surfaces. The Elliptical Geometer would claim
that no lines can be parallel, as they would all converge - the lines of
longitude converge at the Poles. The perpendicular distance between the
'lines' would converge as one moved from the centre -- hence 'ellipse'.

The Hyperbolic Geometer would claim not 1, and not none, but 2 or more
lines could be drawn parallel through the point. The perpendicular
distance between the 'lines' diverged as one moved from the centres,
hence the name 'hyperbolic'- as seen in the shape of the horse-saddle.

**Re Angles in a Triangle:**
$\widehat{A} + \ \widehat{B} + \widehat{C}$ = 180° in Euclid.

The Elliptical model would have
$\widehat{A} + \widehat{B} + \widehat{C}$ $>$ 180°, which is
significant in planning air routes. And the Hyperbolic (the horse
saddle) would have $\widehat{A} + \ \widehat{B} + \widehat{C}$ $<$
180°.

**Re The Sum of Angles of a Rectangle:** For Euclid each
inner angle is 90°. For the Elliptical, three angles were
90° and one larger, whilst the Hyperbolists had three angles as
90°.

**Elliptical Geometers:** **Carl Friedrich Gauss** (1777-
1855) took up the issue. He influenced German **Georg Riemann** (
1826-1866) who claimed 'None' to the question of how many lines could go
through a point off a line but be parallel. His secret was that a
straight line is the arc of a great circle whose centre was NOT on a
piece of flat paper but in a Sphere, as on Earth. This became Spherical
Geometry also called Elliptical Geometry. Riemann contradicted much of
Euclid - and not just the parallel theory. He was immensely shy and
reluctant to shout about his ideas. The circumference of a circle was
less than 2$\pi r$. The curvature was positive -- elliptical -- so the
distance between supposed parallel lines converged. Einstein based his
Theory of Relativity in part on Riemann's model.

***Hyperbolic Geometers:*** The whole idea of laying out
Axioms was questioned. A new Geometry could exist if it denied what had
been claimed. So they denied that one line can be drawn through a point
parallel to a given line. **David Hilbert** (1862 - 1943) -- Professor
at Gottingen - supported these ideas and challenged Euclid. **Janos
Bolyai** (Hungary 1802 to 1860) and **Nickolai Lobachevsky** (Russian
Mathematician 1792 to 1856), denied that only 1 line could be drawn
through a point parallel to a given line. They said that 'Two' lines
could be drawn. This was the Hyperbolic view. As Bolyai said: 'to see an
entire new universe'. It is not radically different from Euclidean
Geometry, but different from Elliptical Geometry. The curvature was
negative.

**MOBIUS STRIPS TOPIC 149**

August Ferdinand Mobius (1790 to 1868) and Johan Listing (1808 to 1882)
almost simultaneously realised something known to the Romans -- twisting
a strip of paper to observe the number of edges and the number of
surfaces. Mobius was an astronomer. At one stage Karl Gauss was his
lecturer. Mobius focussed on 1 twist - some people call it a
half-twist - of a strip of paper, and Listing, who was the first to use
the word Topology, experimented with more than 1 twist.

Instruction (Make a few of each of the samples -- with one twist; then 2
, 3 , 4 twists).

![](/assets/new-dimensions-in-shape-image38.png)*CMB*

- A MOBIUS STRIP HAS ONE TWIST -- ACTUALLY A 'HALF-TWIST'.

Sample 1: From a folio sheet, cut a thin strip about 4 cm
wide, the length of a folio sheet. Glue the short ends together, with A,
B onto C D respectively. This will be known as the ***No-Twist Strip.***

Sample 2: Take a similar strip, but this time twist it and
then glue the ends. A on D, and B on C. This will be the O***ne Twist
Strip*** coil, or ***the Mobius Strip***.

Sample 3: Take a similar longer strip but this time, twist
it twice. Glue the ends.

Sample 4: It is up to you if you want to go further with 3
or 4 or more twists. These are not called 'Mobius' strips. A Mobius
strip has one twist. Johann Listing was to explore many twists, that are
fascinatingly interesting.

- To each of these Samples, perform the following 3 experiments, and
  record your results -- better still, try to anticipate your results.

**Experiment 1:** Hold a pencil on the inside -- halfway
between A and B, and go right round. The pencil lines will probably meet
each other. So how many sides (surfaces) has 'the paper'
got?

**Experiment 2:** Hold a pencil on the inside -- a third
of the way between B and A, so that you can track the edge. Go right
round the loop. The pencil lines will probably meet each other. So how
many edges has the strip got?

**Experiment 3**: With a pair of scissors, cut the strip
(as glued) down the exact middle, from a point midway between A and D
until you have gone right round. Anticipate and record what you get. Do
this to EACH of the samples in turn, trying to anticipate what you will
find.

**Experiment 4:** With a pair of scissors, cut the strip
(as glued) down from a third of the way from B to A, until you have done
the circuit. Anticipate and record what you get. How many edges has this
strip got? Repeat for all 4 samples.

***SOLUTIONS THE MOBIUS STRIP TOPIC 149***

**Sample 1: No twists**

If we take a long strip of rectangular paper ABCD, with long sides AC
and BD - untwisted - and small side AB was glued on to CD to make a loop
without twisting, there would be 2 surfaces (the inside and the
outside), and two edges AC and BD. Try this using a crayon -- going
round. One surface was coloured in and the other was not.

If you cut midway (between A and B) or if you cut a third of the way
(between A and B) right round you would end up with two similar circular
loops. You could of course trace along these paths first with a crayon
and cut later with a pair of scissors. So far, there is nothing
exciting. That is what we expected, as a page has 2 sides (2 faces), and
it has 2 edges. Do the tracing and do the cutting, a third of the way.
Did anything different occur?

**Sample 2: One twist**

Now take AB, twist it (a half-twist) and glue A onto D and B to C. This
is all Augustus Mobius was to do. Johann Listing was to do 2
half-twists, then 3, then 4 and more studying the different effects.

From the middle of AB, place your pen on the now twisted loop, and go
right round. Keep your pen pressing on the face of the page. You will
sooner or later come back to where you started. Hooray. You drew a line
on the upper face. Loosen the glue. This Sample 2 had only one surface,
the one you drew a line on. Do you agree? How many surfaces did the
single-twisted coil actually have? Is this what you expected.

Now, starting at that midway point, cut right round. What would you
expect? How many units of paper would be in your hands -- one or two?
Describe them.

Now, with your pen a third of the way, trace along the 'edge' between A
and B, till the circuit is completed. You found you had only one edge.
Or did you? But loosen the glue, and see what you have. Did you colour
just 'one' edge? With a scissors cut from midway AB right round the
circuit. Try to forecast what you will get. One big coil. How many
'edges' did your loop have.

When you cut along the edge a third of the way from A to B, so that you
are trimming the edge, predict and record what will result. What would
you get if you completed cutting one-third of the way? Two coils -- a
small circuit with a big twist, and another twice its size.Now, both
Listing and Mobius were distorting what they started with and using glue
so their new 'shape' had different properties to the untwisted coil.

**Sample 3: Repeat with 2, with 3, with 4, with 5 twists**

See if there are patterns with odd number of twists versus even numbers
of twists. This was the work of Johann Listing. If you are a Maths
teacher , Google 'Listing', You will be astounded and say : "Thanks for
the lead."

There are 3 primary ways of tessellating.

**Translating** where one, as with tiling, goes from left
to right, or top to bottom -- consecutively, placing one 'tile' next to

**Reflecting** where we can reflect the shape with itself

shape would reflect on itself (on any particular side). One can use a
given side as the mirror, or choose a different 'mirror'.

**Rotating** where the shape spins round a determined axis
-- on or off that sketch.

> Simple tessellations can be any one of these.
>
> Complex tessellations are when 2 or 3 of these 3 transformations are
> combined.
