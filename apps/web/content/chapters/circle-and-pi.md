**AREA OF A 'RECTANGLE':**

Archimedes, regarded as one of the greatest minds ever, determined $\pi$
as between 3$\frac{10}{71}$ and 3$\frac{10}{70}$.. He divided a circle
into any number of sectors, and laid them out to approximate to a
rectangle whose length was half the circumference and whose breadth was
the radius, with the area being 'equal' enough to that of the circle -
making allowance for the concept of limits that was later expounded by
Pierre Fermat (1607 -- 1665) -- and used by Newton and by Liebniz, that
were in time to be used in developing the concept of "LIMITS" as used in
the Calculus of Newton.

would generate a greater level of accuracy. It was a way of fathoming
the infinitesimal. Newton adopted a few ideas like tangents, and turning
points, as well as ways of finding the area under curves from Fermat
without admitting it.

**EXHAUSTION OF THE SUM OF SQUARES**

Another method tried by Archimedes was to determine the Area of a Circle
by the Method of Exhaustion of squares. He sought if there was a
relationship between the areas of the successive squares, or the
successive 'gaps'.

This was to be used by Leibniz and by Newton in determining "the Area
Under the Curve" in developing INTEGRAL Calculus.
