GEOMETRY QUESTIONS FROM WAY BACK TOPIC 147

Euclidean Geometry was synthesised by the Alexandrian Greek Euclid (circa 300 years before Christ). He did not propose a model of the universe. This was done by the Indians and Arabian world who developed Trigonometry to understand the 3-dimensional world they encountered in the sky. Euclid developed rather a link between what can appear on a plane (a flat surface) and with solids. It is actually a system of logic that enables us to deduce logical conclusions to stated premises. The skill of “ If …. Then …. “. He needed a starting point – and the tools of logic.

He proposed 5 axioms (self-evident facts) from which he synthesized what he we know as Euclidean Geometry. With Einstein’s different vision, other Geometries have been postulated. We will look at these.

These 5 axioms embraced that from a POINT, a line can be drawn, and if that line moved in the same direction, it would be straight. If one point of a line rotated around the other that was fixed, a circle would be drawn. Another axiom was that all right angles were equal. Of course, he worked in the 2-Dimensional world.

But the famous, or infamous FIFTH POSTULATE was to prove a weakness. He stated that if 2 lines were cut by a third, then if the 2 inner angles added to less than 180∘\circ∘, those 2 lines would meet. Others were to re-word this postulate as: “If a line passed through a point not on a line, so that it any point on that path is equidistant from the line, then the 2 lines resulting are parallel”.

This was to prove to be the Achilles heel. Thereafter Euclid went into logically provable tenets about triangles, quadrilaterals, etc.

THE WEAK SPOT IN EUCLIDEAN GEOMETRY TOPIC 148

Euclid attempted to create a completely logical system that ‘reflected the physical world’- the flat earth, the work on paper – in 2 Dimensions, rather than the astronomical world as favoured in India and the Middle East. To his credit, he did not purport to be a cosmologist, but rather to be a Logician.

You have read about The 5th Postulate as the weakness in Euclid. Going on to parallel lines, he took another jump – called a logical leap.

We hold that if any 2 parallel lines are cut by a transversal

(i) the corresponding angles are equal (ii) the alternate angles are equal and

(iii) the co-interior angles are supplementary. He did not PROVE these from any earlier premise – but rather as a closed system. This had implications, and eventually he was to be questioned. Never was it proven that the ‘corresponding angles are equal’. It has been stated, and used to proceed to other 2 statements.

All Mathematics teachers survive the questioning child by making the pupils learn the Theorem off by heart. So the next generation misses out questioning the tenets proposed. His only way of silencing the critic is to claim that this is a closed system.

If the corresponding angles are equal, then the other 2 facts are provable.

If the alternate angles are equal, then the other 2 facts are provable.

If the co-interior angles are supplementary, then the other 2 facts are equal.

But Euclid is forever criticised by that FIFTH POSTULATE: If one had a straight line, and a point off that line, then the line drawn through the point, equidistant from the given line, would show that the 2 lines are parallel on to infinity. Was he correct? Think? It was to be regarded as a weak spot in his arguments. Why? Why? Why?

NON-EUCLIDEAN GEOMETRY

So it was not sacred that 2 lines equidistant from each other are parallel. It was to be found that the angles of a triangle necessarily add to 180∘.\circ.∘. Nor that the circumference of a circle was 2π\piπr. Apparently. Euclidean lines had no curvature. It was drawn on flat paper. The frame of reference was flat – a piece of 2-dimensional paper (Image 343). All that is true if the surface was 2-Dimensional – on flat paper.

But are surfaces only ‘FLAT”? Think of a sphere. Think of a horse saddle. Can we make those statements on parallel lines, and on triangles?

On a spherical shape, like the ‘global’ earth (Image 344) that was being increasingly mapped, and where air flights were planned, that path could look ‘straight’ if you look from above, but if you viewed from the side, it was curved.

To go from one airport to another, the shortest distance was not from mapping, but by tracking ‘great circles’. Parallel lines (like the lines of longitude) would converge at one point (North or South Pole), so there were no parallel lines.

The angles of a triangle swelled to be more than 180∘\circ∘. The circumference of a circle would be less than 2π\piπr. The ‘perpendicular’ distance between 2 points, was not constant, but reduced as the lines converged to a point. Thus it was called Elliptical (Spherical) Geometry. Lines on an Elliptical shape had a positive curvature, converging – as we see at the North and South Poles.

The Islamic Mathematicians tried to put Euclid on a more solid basis. Ibn Al-Haytham (965-1039) redefined parallelism as equidistance. Then Omar Khayyam, as Umar Ibn Ibrahim al-Khayyami (1048-1123) took a different approach, before the ideas of Nasir al-Din al-Tusi (1201-1274) reached England’s John Wallis and the Italian Girolamo Saccheri (1676-1733), who asked the questions – but gave up pushing further.

There are many Geometries. There are primarily 3 that reflect the 2-Dimensional world and 8 that reflect the 3-Dimensional World. It depends on what our model of the physical world is – mainly combinations of the basic three below. At school level, we sweat on the Euclidean Geometry. Why?

Taking that third type of surface – the horse saddle – ‘the hyperbola’ - if one tried to draw parallel lines (equally distant from point to the line) the lines would not stay equally far apart (as expected in Euclidean Geometry), nor would they converge ( as in Elliptical Geometry), but they would diverge.

On these 3 surfaces, respectively, the sum of the angles would equal 180∘,\ 180\circ, 180∘,exceed 180∘\circ∘, or be less than 180∘\circ∘ - as seen in these sketches!

Re: Parallel lines: Given a parallel line, and a point P away from that line AB, Euclid had it that there was 1 and only one line that could be drawn through P parallel to AB, and the distance between the 2 lines was constant infinitely. Try to draw ‘parallel’ lines on each of these 3 surfaces. The Elliptical Geometer would claim that no lines can be parallel, as they would all converge - the lines of longitude converge at the Poles. The perpendicular distance between the ‘lines’ would converge as one moved from the centre – hence ‘ellipse’.

The Hyperbolic Geometer would claim not 1, and not none, but 2 or more lines could be drawn parallel through the point. The perpendicular distance between the ‘lines’ diverged as one moved from the centres, hence the name ‘hyperbolic’- as seen in the shape of the horse-saddle.

Re Angles in a Triangle: A^+ B^+C^\widehat{A} + \ \widehat{B} + \widehat{C}A+ B+C = 180∘\circ∘ in Euclid.

The Elliptical model would have A^+B^+C^\widehat{A} + \widehat{B} + \widehat{C}A+B+C >>> 180∘\circ∘, which is significant in planning air routes. And the Hyperbolic (the horse saddle) would have A^+ B^+C^\widehat{A} + \ \widehat{B} + \widehat{C}A+ B+C <<< 180∘\circ∘.

Re The Sum of Angles of a Rectangle: For Euclid each inner angle is 90∘\circ∘. For the Elliptical, three angles were 90∘\circ∘ and one larger, whilst the Hyperbolists had three angles as 90∘\circ∘.

Elliptical Geometers: Carl Friedrich Gauss (1777- 1855) took up the issue. He influenced German Georg Riemann ( 1826-1866) who claimed ‘None’ to the question of how many lines could go through a point off a line but be parallel. His secret was that a straight line is the arc of a great circle whose centre was NOT on a piece of flat paper but in a Sphere, as on Earth. This became Spherical Geometry also called Elliptical Geometry. Riemann contradicted much of Euclid - and not just the parallel theory. He was immensely shy and reluctant to shout about his ideas. The circumference of a circle was less than 2πr\pi rπr. The curvature was positive – elliptical – so the distance between supposed parallel lines converged. Einstein based his Theory of Relativity in part on Riemann’s model.

Hyperbolic Geometers: The whole idea of laying out Axioms was questioned. A new Geometry could exist if it denied what had been claimed. So they denied that one line can be drawn through a point parallel to a given line. David Hilbert (1862 - 1943) – Professor at Gottingen - supported these ideas and challenged Euclid. Janos Bolyai (Hungary 1802 to 1860) and Nickolai Lobachevsky (Russian Mathematician 1792 to 1856), denied that only 1 line could be drawn through a point parallel to a given line. They said that ‘Two’ lines could be drawn. This was the Hyperbolic view. As Bolyai said: ‘to see an entire new universe’. It is not radically different from Euclidean Geometry, but different from Elliptical Geometry. The curvature was negative.

Distraction : The First Successful Television

Image 377 Image 378 Image 379

Since the invention of the wireless radio, a number of people tried to transmit pictures. Sound could be changed to electricity. Then Henri Becquerel showed that light could be transformed to electricity. Many scientists tried to break through, but Scotsman John Logie Baird (1888 to 1946) was well placed, being in London. He raised money by selling soap and socks. The first success is attributed to him. This was achieved in 1926, on January 26th. He showed two ventriloquist’s dummies in action. Two years later – using phone lines, he transmitted from London to New York. The first successful colour television was later able to transmit across the Atlantic. He was sickly, yet in 1924 he survived an 1000 Volt shock. He hoped that the Daily Express newspaper would help him promote his idea, but the Editor thought he was mad trying to send photos by radio. “Get rid of him. He most probably has a razor on him”. The Manchester Guardian said that no good would come of it. Sadly, the Marconi Company produced 450 lines to Baird’s 250, so the B.B.C. adopted Marconi’s system, and this after Baird started using the Cathode Ray receiver – invented by Ferdinand Braun. He continued to explore, but died of a heart attack in 1946, aged 58. He was honoured in many ways after his death.

MOBIUS STRIPS TOPIC 149

August Ferdinand Mobius (1790 to 1868) and Johan Listing (1808 to 1882) almost simultaneously realised something known to the Romans – twisting a strip of paper to observe the number of edges and the number of surfaces. Mobius was an astronomer. At one stage Karl Gauss was his lecturer. Mobius focussed on 1 twist - some people call it a half-twist - of a strip of paper, and Listing, who was the first to use the word Topology, experimented with more than 1 twist.

SOLUTIONS THE MOBIUS STRIP TOPIC 149

Sample 1: No twists

If we take a long strip of rectangular paper ABCD, with long sides AC and BD - untwisted - and small side AB was glued on to CD to make a loop without twisting, there would be 2 surfaces (the inside and the outside), and two edges AC and BD. Try this using a crayon – going round. One surface was coloured in and the other was not.

If you cut midway (between A and B) or if you cut a third of the way (between A and B) right round you would end up with two similar circular loops. You could of course trace along these paths first with a crayon and cut later with a pair of scissors. So far, there is nothing exciting. That is what we expected, as a page has 2 sides (2 faces), and it has 2 edges. Do the tracing and do the cutting, a third of the way. Did anything different occur?

Sample 2: One twist

Now take AB, twist it (a half-twist) and glue A onto D and B to C. This is all Augustus Mobius was to do. Johann Listing was to do 2 half-twists, then 3, then 4 and more studying the different effects.

From the middle of AB, place your pen on the now twisted loop, and go right round. Keep your pen pressing on the face of the page. You will sooner or later come back to where you started. Hooray. You drew a line on the upper face. Loosen the glue. This Sample 2 had only one surface, the one you drew a line on. Do you agree? How many surfaces did the single-twisted coil actually have? Is this what you expected.

Now, starting at that midway point, cut right round. What would you expect? How many units of paper would be in your hands – one or two? Describe them.

Now, with your pen a third of the way, trace along the ‘edge’ between A and B, till the circuit is completed. You found you had only one edge. Or did you? But loosen the glue, and see what you have. Did you colour just ‘one’ edge? With a scissors cut from midway AB right round the circuit. Try to forecast what you will get. One big coil. How many ‘edges’ did your loop have.

When you cut along the edge a third of the way from A to B, so that you are trimming the edge, predict and record what will result. What would you get if you completed cutting one-third of the way? Two coils – a small circuit with a big twist, and another twice its size.Now, both Listing and Mobius were distorting what they started with and using glue so their new ‘shape’ had different properties to the untwisted coil.

Sample 3: Repeat with 2, with 3, with 4, with 5 twists

See if there are patterns with odd number of twists versus even numbers of twists. This was the work of Johann Listing. If you are a Maths teacher , Google ‘Listing’, You will be astounded and say : ”Thanks for the lead.”


MAURITZ CORNELIUS ESCHER 1898 to 1972 TOPIC 150

A sickly child, and unhappy at school, Escher came into his own at the School of Decorative Design in Den Hague. Escher used many Mathematical ideas, like symmetry and tessellations (filling space), and later he was into topology. In his travels to Italy he saw how the little country houses held up to the occasional tremors, but it was the details of the Moorish art in Granada (Spain) that fascinated him. He made lithographs and woodcuttings, and it was only at the end of his life that he was recognised as an artist, having just been seen as an intellectual who played games. He had had no one ti be compared to, so was not taken as an artist.

In the Art world, a singular person like Escher, or South Africa’s leather artist Beatrix Bosch, takes a while to get recognised as there is nobody to compare to – worldwide!

Many people think that Escher was employed in the post office. Not true, but he did design stamps at one stage for the Netherlands Postal Services. As a youngster, he studied insects and little creatures and used them in his cuttings – even emerging from the picture! Shades of Gaudi – inspired by raw nature.

There are 3 primary ways of tessellating.

Translating where one, as with tiling, goes from left to right, or top to bottom – consecutively, placing one ‘tile’ next to the other – side by side.

Reflecting where we can reflect the shape with itself or with a chosen line. Obviously if we want to cover the full area, the shape would reflect on itself (on any particular side). One can use a given side as the mirror, or choose a different ‘mirror’.

Rotating where the shape spins round a determined axis – on or off that sketch.

Simple tessellations can be any one of these.

Complex tessellations are when 2 or 3 of these 3 transformations are combined.
