**THE CALCULATOR'S "DRG" KEY TOPIC 69**

You saw earlier that Phi = $\varnothing$ = 2 cos 36° =
1,618033..........The Golden Ratio.

You are aware that there are 360° in a full rotation (a circle)
and that the right angle is quarter of the way, being $90°$

After the French Revolution, many units (in Europe) were made to be
METRIC.

Thus the Right Angle was called 'One Hundred GRAD' meaning that the Full
Rotation was 400 grad or 400 gon, written as $400^{g}.$ Grads have
caused problems.

How? Think.

![](/assets/trigonometry-image4.png)*LCB*

***\*\*\*\* N.B. : ANOTHER NAME FOR GRAD IS 'GON'***

1 Gon = 10 decigons (dgon) = 100 centigon (Cgon)

1 Degree = 60 minutes = 3600 seconds. In a circle, 360 degrees = 400
gon.

Degrees and Grads (or Gons) are measureable 'quantifiable' units, but a
RADIAN is a pure QUALITATIVE number (a ratio), so
at University where you will do Pure Mathematics, you will use RADIANS
so that there are pure numbers on each of the x-axis and the y-axis,
particularly significant when the inverse function is also drawn.

For a Radian, the RADIUS is placed anti-clockwise along the
circumference of the circle from zero. The arc will be equal to the
radius. The angle thus made is called 1 Radian. Thus 3 Radians will be
nearly halfway round the circumference. Halfway will be $\pi$ radians.
Think. A QUANTITATIVE number tells us the number
of units involved, eg 6 as in 6 cm; 16 as in 16°C.

In fact, a full Rotation is just more than 6 Radians, being 2$\pi$
Radians. Thus at 180° we sit on $\pi\ \ Radians.\ $ Thus
**90**$\mathbf{\ degrees}$ **is 100 grad is**
$\frac{\mathbf{\pi}}{\mathbf{2}}$ **Radians.**

**THE STORY OF "SINCOSTANCOSECSECCOT" TOPIC 70**

**T*his is a topic dear to me. I taught the 1982 Matric class at
Fairmont High School by investigating Perfect Figures. We were to
'discover' this topic that had never been explained in any textbook. At
a crucial moment one Colin Ledgerwood came up with the trump suggestion.
Obviously someone must have devised this, but like so many issues in
School Mathematics, one is simply 'Told' -- as a gospel tenet.***

*It could well have been India's Madhava Sangamagrama (1340 to 1425) who
understood the various trigonometrical inter-relationships, though the
Arabs were using all 6 trigonometrical relationships in the 10^th^
Century.*

*Aryabhaty in the 6^th^ Century and Hipparchus of Nicea in 160 B.C. knew
some of these relationships. These days, one just needs to Google!*

If there are 3 people in a family -- Father, Mother, Child -- there are
obviously 6 different relationships. F can rule M, F can rule C and M
can rule F, M can rule C. Also C can rule F and C can rule M. Subtle
relationships -- but 6 of them.

In a right angled triangle ABC with B = 90$°$ we will focus on
angle A, and refer to AC as the Hypotenuse, refer to BC as the Opposite
side to angle A, and refer to AB as the side Adjacent to angle A . So
the 3 sides can relate in 6 different ways.

![](/assets/trigonometry-image9.png)*LCB*

Specific names -- for specific reasons - have been given to each of
these ratios;

sinusoidal; co-sinusoidal; tangential; co-tangential; secantial and
co-secantial.

You will soon learn the meaning of these words -- 'sin', cos', 'tan',
'cosec', 'sec' and 'cot' which at this moment seem totally odd-ball
words to us.

First, let's get an idea on Trigonometry.

\* Consider the Right Angled Triangle ABC, with Angle B = 90°

The 6 relationships are known as

sin A = $\frac{opposite}{hypoteneuse}$ SOH cosec A =
$\frac{1}{\sin A}\ \ \ \ $ = $\frac{hypoteneuse}{opposite}$

$\frac{1}{\cos A}\ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \  = \ \ \frac{hypoteneuse}{adjacent}$

tan A = $\frac{opposite}{adjacent}$ TOA cot A = $\frac{1}{\tan A}$ =
$\frac{adjacent}{opposite}$

In the general RIGHT-ANGLED triangle

sin A = $\frac{BC}{AC}$ = $\frac{a}{b}$ cosec A = $\frac{BC}{AC}$ =
$\frac{b}{a}$ (can be called "csc A")

cos A = $\frac{AB}{AC}$ = $\frac{c}{b}$ sec A = $\frac{AC}{AB}$ =
$\frac{b}{c}$

tan A = $\frac{BC}{AB}$ = $\frac{a}{c}$ cot A = $\frac{AB}{BC}$ =
$\frac{c}{a}$

$\frac{1}{\tan A}$ = $\frac{\cos A}{\sin A}$
