**THE DARTBOARD - THE GAME 301, 501 or 101 TOPIC 154**

![](/assets/playing-by-the-rules-image4.jpeg)

*Image 421*

The dartboard holds the numbers 1 to 20. There are 2 small inner 'bulls'
-- the inner bull is worth a score of 50 (Bull's Eye), and the outer
bull is worth 25. There is a halfway ring that trebles the score, and an
outer ring where the score is doubled, If you throw 3 inners, that will
give you a bonus score of 150.

Question 1: In the game called 301, to start, you must
throw a double (in the outer green -- red ring), and to end, you must
also throw a double -- in that same outer ring. What happens in between
is up to you. You must end on EXACTLY ZERO !

If your first throw was a 16. No luck. It must be a double -- in the
outer ring. You throw double 19 (in the outer ring). So subtract: 301 --
2(19) = 263. Now you throw a 5 and a treble 17. So you are down to 207.
Proceed until what score? "ZERO."

Say you threw a treble 20, and then a 15. Your score would be reduced to
132, so you do not need to throw an odd number. Why? Throwing a double
16 takes you to 100. Can you score 100 exactly in your next 2 or 3
throws? Attempt an answer.

Question 2: Suppose you are in a hurry and want to play
"101" to the same rules. Is it possible to complete your 101 in 3 moves?
What number(s) would you throw?

Darts were played by members of the British Army and the Navy to while
away idle time. Henry VIII took it seriously. In 1896, Brian Gamlin, a
Lancashire carpenter, established the modern game with the number layout
we get today. The pubs in London seem to produce the champions.

The current World Champion (2024) is Luke Humphries who defeated 16 year
old prodigy Luke Littler 7- 4, after trailing 2 - 4. Littler later threw
a "Nine Dart". This means he scored 501 with the minimum of 9 darts --
Seven treble 20s, a treble 19 and a double 12. Woman champ in 2023 is 19
year old Beay Greaves of England who won the English Open in 2016 as a
12 year old.

**THE SIGN LAWS VIA THE DARTBOARD TOPIC 155**

\* I gave this talk to the AMESA Mathematics Conference of 1994 in
Bloemfontein.

Each white number on a dartboard was taken as negative, and each black
number was positive. Doubles, trebles and bulls are included and operate
as normal. Each throw must score. Teams can be chosen, with each thrower
suggesting his total and being challenged, e.g. "I am aiming for treble
17". Penalise a team if they make an arithmetic error. 3 darts are
thrown by each member in turn, referred to as a, b, c.

In some contests, try to score the highest, in some, try to score the
lowest.

Stage1: a + b + c = (first dart) + (second dart) + (third
dart).

Use the brackets ( ) + ( ) + ( ), then remove brackets and then record
the Score. Encourage players to nominate what they are going for. Play
many games.

Pupil 1 throws 3, 10 and 4. Score is (3) + (10) + (-4) = 3 +10 - 4 = 9.

Pupil 2 throws 20, 2(19) and 18. Score is (20) + 2 (-19) ) + (18 ) =
20 - 38 + 18 = 0.

Hence surprisingly Pupil 1's team won the first game by 9 points.

Stage 2: 2a + $b^{2}$ + 3c. Proceed. Eg 2(2 ) + (-1
$)^{2}$ + 3 (-16 ) etc. Think.

Stage 3: a + b -- c = ( ) + ( ) - ( ).

They want to win, so let the pupils fight out the -- (- 6). Do you Win
or Lose? Argue!

\* TAKE THE TROUBLE TO CONTROL THE BRACKETS SLOWLY.

Then at Stage 4 we can change formula to 3a -- 2b - $c^{2}$. Think . 3(
) -- 2( ) -- ( $)^{2}$

Before a pupil throws, he/she should nominate, and say "I want to throw
a small positive", or "a big negative". This anticipation is vital, and
speeds up pupil's perceptions. Have at least one of the contests with
this formula to be decided by the lowest score as the winner -- but all
darts must score.

Stage 4 could be 3ab - 2$b^{3}c$ - 4c

Stage 5: When I gave the presentation in Bloemfontein, I
challenged anyone in the audience to only use a, b and c once each to
make a winning formula with as high a number as possible/. One fellow
came up and wrote on the board

$$(a^{b})^{c}$$

Well, he started with a negative, like ' -19'. Frown. Then another
negative like -17, so his offering was first $({( - 19)}^{b})^{c}$ . SAD
indeed. Cry! $({( - 19)}^{- 17})^{c}$ is even more sad. Tears flowed.
$\frac{1}{({- 19)}^{17}}$ . Can you get worse?

'Negative the reciprocal of 5,48 x $10^{21}$ '.What will the 'c' value

Pride was smashed. And then slowly, slowly, slowly, the group started
anticipating and coaching the young guy as to what to throw, so that he
could be excitingly happy. Eyes opened. Must 'c' be positive or
negative, big or small, odd or even?

The young guy took the advice and won, getting a remarkable score.

$({( - 19)}^{- 17})^{- 16}$ = (-19$)^{+ 272}$ = positive AND too big for
the calculator to record!

That is what we call a high score, and he threw 3 big negatives!

By now, the audience was on a high.

***Game Kit 1: Roulette***

A popular Casino game that pays 36 to 1 or other odds depending on where
you place your chip -- and if your number comes up. (36 + your money
back = 37).

But if the ball stops at 0, then the Banker (dealer) keeps all the
money.

One can bet at 1 to 1, if a Black or if a Red number will come up, as
you guess. There are quite a few other combinations that one can bet on.
And what of the new 00?

\* For this exercise we will concentrate on betting on 3 rows or 1
column of numbers that offers odds of 2 to 1 if any of the numbers in
that row or column are lucky. There are three 2 to 1 columns. The
Betting is 2 to 1 on each of these columns.

- The bad news is that the Betting Houses might know the Mathematics, so
