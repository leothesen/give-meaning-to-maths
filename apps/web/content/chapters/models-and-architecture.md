**PRAGMATICS**

When jet engines were first used in aeroplanes, passengers smiled. The
Jet-Set was created. And then there was a fatal crash off the Italian
coast in 1953 with 35 deaths. On investigating, the fault was seen in
the rectangular windows. Their frames could not take the stress
pressures and basically 'collapsed' in what was first called 'metal
fatigue'. Windows in aeroplanes now are rounded in the 'corners'.

**Designing Arches**

The Romans were at their height at the time of Julius Caesar. They
wanted tin used to make their armour and weapons and the wheels of their
chariots and it was in Britain that they found the tin. Thus they built
roads through Gaul (France). Ruthless fighting ensued, but also
road-building and bridge-building, so that the armies could move and
grape vines were planted to provide the medicine for its army's health.
The Romans used Self-Supporting Arches (no cement) - Ideal for bridges.

**Gaudi of Barcelona**

Antoni Gaudi was born in 1852, and spent his spare time as a boy
studying the structure of dessert-like plants that was to later be
expressed in his style with an almost absence of straight lines. He only
just passed at the University and it was said that he would not succeed.
He went through a period of religious rebellion.

In 1882 the architect del Villa was chosen to build a unique cathedral
devoted to The Holy Family (Jesus, Mary and Joseph) -- the Sacrada
Familia. St Joseph is the patron saint of workers, and thus the patron
saint of Barcelona whose people had no love of the Spanish rulers in
Madrid, having lost their independence in 1505.

A year later del Villa resigned and 31-year old Gaudi took over for the
next 44 years. He devoted himself fully to the cause and his religious
spirit deepened. Returning home one day, he was run over by a bus and
died. Because he looked like a tramp, he was given no attention and was
being prepared to be buried as a hobo. You need to see Gaudi's work --
even on the Internet to be totally fascinated.

*In designing the spires, he made a motif with cotton weights that he
hung upside down, until it balanced. Inverted, it became the shape of
one of the 4 sides of the incomplete masterpiece. (See below)*

The Sacrada has 4 aspects -- North, South, East, West, and is far from
complete.

**Spires**

Architecture is an incredible study. If you go to Bordeaux in France,
you see a Spire built in the garden next to the church -- St Michael -
and not atop the church. Why?

The city is built on the bank of the navigable Garonne River that is
marshy at certain places. With the movement of river sand, there was a
fear that a certain shift could just be one too many. So the church has
a spire next to it, and not on top.

The famed Tower of Pisa is actually the Bell Tower of the City's
cathedral. Pisa is in an unstable -- due to possible earthquakes -
region in Tuscany.

**Modern Architecture**

Europe suffered many social changes in the 19^th^ and 20^th^ centuries
with wars. Great damage was done -- especially by bombings in 1916 and
in the 1940 -1945 World War. Leadership and new directions in
architecture came from the United States.

Frank Lloyd WRIGHT (1867 to 1959) was the leader of American
Architecture.

![](/assets/models-and-architecture-image62.jpeg) ![](/assets/models-and-architecture-image63.jpeg)

well-received. He was not easy to get on with and married 3 times.

**1000 ml CONTAINERS TOPIC 139**

A teacher could challenge a class to design and construct models of a
cube, of a pyramid (on a square base), a rectangular prism on a square
case, a cone, a cylinder and a sphere whose volume is the same as a 1000
ml milk bottle or 750 ml as a wine bottle. You will need specific
measurements, and to do some calculations.

This is a great Exercise! So see if you can strategize. An extension of
this is to add a touch of design as one would find with perfume bottles,
or cool-drink bottles -- the marketability factor. Be conventional, but
also push convention. Hang as mobiles.

![](/assets/models-and-architecture-image72.jpeg)

Investigate on making an alternate to the 1000 ml bottle. Should it be a
cube? A sphere? A rectangular prism, a rectangular base pyramid, a
cylinder, a cone? Or something more exotic? Some people have the ability
to handle spatial concepts and to strategize. You will see various
shapes on supermarket shelves. Explore.

Determine, also, the Surface Area that you would use in each case.
People in production determine if the contents (volume) are more
expensive than the waxing and printing (surface area) of a product, so
they need to make decisions. Do you want to minimise the volume
(contents) or to minimise the the surface area (printing) in making a
container?

One can always add a little flap on to certain sides so that the unit
can be glued.

You may want to mass--produce these cubes using expensive wax paper,
that has its labels printed. Once you prepare to pass cardboard through
cutting and printing machines in a huge scale, you will see that the
area used is not likely to be 600 ${cm}^{2}$. You are more likely to use
boards of 45 cm by 35 cm to allow for the flaps. Flaps mean the joinings
and glue and certain products -- like milk -- will not handle this.

It would be too expensive and have too many joints and corners. Air
would get in too easily? Instead, buy a one litre milk carton, or
'Tetrapak' to give it the trade name. Perhaps a cooldrink or milk in a
waxed cardboard container - and then open it out. What you find will
surprise you. The package is much more expensive than the contents --
and it has little throw-away value.

The square you find will has all the folding parts grooved in by
machine, making it easy to fold and to 'glue'. No holes for leaking out
or for air to enter. This is clever thinking. There is clever thinking
too to avoid glue. Open such a container.

**The Wonder of the Tetrapak.**

*If you need a litre of milk or pure Fruit Juice, you would want it to
fit into your hand, and to fit in the fridge door. You would want no
leaks, no air getting in. You would not make it 10 cm x 10 cm x 10 cm.

Thus Q(r) = $\frac{3}{r}$ can be drawn as the graph y = $\frac{3}{x}$
(seen below)

We can see that the greater the radius (the smaller berry) the less is
the quality of the wine if all other factors are in place. Your great
wines come from small-berried vines -- the red Cabernet Sauvignon and
the white Chardonnay that ripen late.

In an ideal vintage, the sugars and the skin will ripen simultaneously,
letting the berries lie longer on the vine before harvesting to reach an
ideal ripeness -- hopefully before rain or hail falls. These grapes
would be physiologically ripe.

More often than not, in the heat of summer, the sugars react to the
40° C temperature and soar to high levels that will lead to
high alcohols, but the Skin (the real fruit is just inside that skin)
struggles to reach optimum ripeness. A decision needed!

If he picks at B (on the graph) if the sugars are ripe (he would not
like big alcohol wines) then the skin is not ideally ripe. If he picks
at C, then the Skins are ripe, but he has paid the price of getting high
sugars, that convert to very high alcohols. This is what happened in the
Cape in the hot vintages of 1998 to 2000 -- but the judges at shows were
oblivious and gave high ratings to such wines -- that really needed 20
years' bottle maturation to save the day. What does one do? It goes in
phases.

The better winemakers tend to pick slightly pre-ripe so as to hold lower
alcohols and to build an identifiable taste profile. It takes years of
experience and much restraint for a young winemaker to pick pre-ripe as
his boss wants him to win medals, and thus he will pick full-ripe that
yields higher alcohols. Medals are not won for the best apples, the best
pears, the best spuds -- but are for the best wine!
