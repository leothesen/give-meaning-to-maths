*Our friend Leonhard Euler stood at the Konigsberg Bridge in Russia in
1735, and wondered if he could go for a walk, crossing each bridge, but
not walking on the same bridge more than once. Better still, could he
return to the starting point?*

(a) *Could he do so? Give it a go, and try!*

(b) *If not, what should be done so that he can do so?*

Explanation: There are 4 pieces of land, that can be
represented as a dark Point, called a Node or Vertex -- A; B; C; D. We
will say "V = 4" or V = 4. From A are 5 bridges and 3 from each other
Vertex. There were 4 Faces, the water with a bridge on either side. We
say F = 4. And there were 7 bridges, called Edges, connecting A; B; C;
D. We say "E = 7". He realised that it was impossible to succeed unless
there was an even number of bridges from each node, or there was a
starting node that was odd, and an end node that was odd, but the rest
were even.

Do you see that land A has 5 sides - the bridges (i.e. 5 Edges, 5 lines
emanating from A). Land B, C and D have 3 bridges each. Both 5 and 3 are
odd. In this sketch there are 4 Vertices (sometimes called Nodes, e.g.
A, B, C, D). There 4 Faces. Euler maintained that E + 1 = V + F, that is
seen as true in this case. E+1 = 7+ 1= 8. V + F = 4 + 4 = 8 This is
written better as **V + F - E = 1**. Thus 4 + 4 -- 7 = 1. This
1 was called 'The Euler Character' in all drawings on flat paper -- in a
similar dimension. They were homomorphic.

If you take the letters of the alphabet, then we see O and Q are
homomorphic -- each is one hole, not like B that has 2 holes. The
letters E, F, T, Y all have '3 legs', no holes. Topology has led to
techniques in making cartoon films, planning roadways -- especially in
deciding which country towns to link by road on a national level, or in
planning computer networks, exotic architecture, advertising gimmicks,
or in designing models for biological structures.

In answering: "What is the World?" "What is the Meaning of Life?" "What
is .... ?" they considered the known world -- the earth they were on -
the Sun that they saw each morning, and the far-away stars -- the
Cosmos. Where did the Cosmos end? Why was it there in the first place?
Did the cosmos affect and influence us as humans? What was the
composition of the Cosmos? Were there Gods controlling the variables?

Thales, as the original Cosmologist, stressed the Water aspect. "All is
Water". It was generally felt that the basic elements of the Cosmos were
Earth, Fire, Water, Air -- and or combinations thereof, called Hyle. His
pupil was Anixamander, and his pupil - in turn was Aniximenes (586 BC to
526 BC) of Miletus who stressed the Air aspect. "All is Air". This
represented the world of ideas -- the euphoric. Soon, the Persians
conquered the Greeks so many of these discourses were interrupted, and
became merely academic.

After a few decades of glory, the militaristic Sparta (naturally the
adherents of "All is Fire") defeated the thinking Athenians and Socrates
was put to death. Athenians had the idea of atoms (Democratus) -- the
importance of the individual -- that is the complete opposite to the
Spartan idea of Rule by Force -- by Fire -- by the Despot.

**The Wisest Man**: ***The famed Oracle of Delphi was
asked by the Wise Men: "Who was the wisest of all?" The Oracle said
"Socrates". He (Socrates) would not accept the suggestion, arguing that
those who called him 'wisest' implied that they were regarding
themselves as wise, ( or even more wise, but not most wise!) and that
(praising oneself) was not displaying wisdom. In refusing to call
himself 'wise', he was the only one portraying wisdom. Or was he? Do you
see the twist -- a double twist? So, was Socrates the wisest? His pupil
was the the gifted Plato (428 BC to 348 BC) whose Philosophy, with that
of Aristotle (384BC -- 322BC), basically fashioned Western thinking. St
Thomas Aquinas (1225 to 1274) used Aristoltelian thought as he was more
practical (empirical) than the idealism and abstract thinking of
Plato.***

In 1596, Astronomer Johannes Kepler linked up with Platonic thinking. In
Kepler's effort to explain the distances between the then known planets,
realising that each of them -- modeled as a polyhedron, could fit into a
sphere and have a sphere fitted into them -- with Kepler taking it that
a sphere was to to be a model of the Universe.

At the same time, Plato was a universal thinker and did not want to
stress any one of the 4 known basic elements fire, earth, air and water
above the other. This would have meant that he identified with a
specific philosophical or political group.

Plato was to claim that each of the then known 4 REGULAR POLYHEDRONS
represented one of the elements Fire, Earth, Air and Water that would
differ from the Aether -- a 5^th^ element, that incorporated and
superceded the other 4.

In a famed discourse, he modelled Fire as the Tetrahedron (the upright
flame), Earth (the least mobile) as the Cube (in between mobility), Air

D4: **First a little story:**

*Sir Isaac Newton, in the 16^th^ century, regarded the concepts of mass,
distance and time (and hence distance per time that is speed - or
velocity - and velocity per second (that is acceleration) as
**ABSOLUTE** concepts. The full, final and unchangeable
explanations of the universe! He stated 3 laws. An object would continue
in a static position, or continue moving until it is influenced by
another force. For every action, there is an equal and opposite
reaction. His 3^rd^ law measured Force as Mass times Acceleration,
written as **F = ma**. In effect he defined FORCE in terms of the 3
'absolutes' that -- to him -- governed the heavenly bodies: mass,
distance, time. This led to the Mechanical Age and The Industrial
Revolution - that was to see Britain covered with factories and ships
sailing the seas.*

*In the 1870s the research of Michael Faraday and James Maxwell taught
man about electricity and its link to magnetism. The phenomenon of
electro-magnetism led to the development of cars and even more factory
workshops and aeroplanes. Maxwell spoke of The Indestructibility of
Matter, and determined that electro-magnetic waves travel at what we now
call 'the speed of light' -- very close to 300 x* $10^{6}$ *kilometres
per second. Over 7 times round the earth in a second! Albert Einstein,
in 1905, in a hurry, wrote down E = m*$c^{2}$*, where he regarded these
very concepts not as Absolutes but as **RELATIVE** and
spoke of The Indestructibility of Energy to add to Maxwell's
Indestructibility of Mass. He showed the link between Energy and Mass.
One would change to the other.*

*For the astute amongst you, he meant to write* $\frac{Energy}{Mass}$ *=
(speed of light*$)^{2}$ *but he was in a hurry and wrote E = m*$c^{2}$*,
making it tougher to understand what he really meant. If each of Energy
and Mass were indestructible, then when we 'see' mass being apparently
destroyed, it is 'energy' being released. This was the idea of the
atomic Bomb, and of the creation of galaxies -- the life of stars.*

*And 'mass' would form if 'energy' seemed to be destroyed -- what
happens in the life of stars and galaxies.*

See Question D4 on this page.
