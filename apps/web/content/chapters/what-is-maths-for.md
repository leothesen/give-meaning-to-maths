A working answer to the most common question in the classroom.

There is a particular silence that falls over a Year 9 maths classroom when a
pupil, with the air of someone springing a trap, asks *Sir, when am I ever
going to use this?* The silence is not really a silence — it is twenty-eight
other pupils suddenly listening very carefully, because they want to hear what
you say.

The honest answer is that most of them, in fact, will not use the quadratic
formula again. They will not factorise a polynomial in their adult lives. If
"use" means "deploy in a paid context", then a great deal of school
mathematics is, by that measure, useless.

But this is the wrong question.

## What we actually teach

When we teach a child to solve `2x + 5 = 11` we are not, in truth, teaching
them to solve *that* equation. We are teaching them three things at once:

- that a problem can be written down in symbols and rearranged on the page;
- that there is a procedure that can be checked, step by step;
- that, at the end, an answer either works or it does not, and there is no
  appeal.

These are not maths skills. These are habits of mind. And the curious thing
about habits of mind is that they leak. The pupil who learns to rearrange an
equation also, quietly, learns to rearrange an argument.

## The other answer

There is a second answer, which I give to the older pupils when I think they
will sit still for it. It is that mathematics is one of the very few subjects
in which a fourteen-year-old can be *right* in a way that the headmaster is
not, and the Prime Minister is not, and the most expensive lawyer in the
country is not.

A correct proof is correct. It does not become more correct because a famous
person agrees, and it does not become less correct because nobody does. For a
teenager — surrounded, as teenagers are, by adults whose authority is mostly a
matter of *because I said so* — this is electric. It is the first place in
their schooling where the question "says who?" has a clean answer: *says the
argument*.

> Mathematics is the place in the curriculum where the truth does not depend
> on who is in the room.

That is what mathematics is for. The rest — the formulae, the techniques, the
apparatus — is scaffolding. Useful scaffolding, often beautiful scaffolding,
but scaffolding all the same. The building is the habit of clear thought.
